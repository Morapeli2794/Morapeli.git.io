Week 3: Computer-Aided Design (CAD)

Date: 14 to 21 Oct

Quick Summary

  • Skills Learned: During Week 3 I focused on developing practical design skills using CorelDRAW as the primary tool for Computer-Aided Design (CAD). The week emphasized vector-based design techniques essential for digital fabrication processes such as laser cutting and vinyl plotting. Participants learned how to create precise and scalable graphics using CorelDRAW’s drawing tools, manage layers and object alignment for organized design workflows, and integrate typography effectively into their layouts. They also gained experience in preparing fabrication-ready files by understanding line weights, color coding for cut paths, and exporting in compatible formats like DXF and SVG.   • Digital Tools and Software Used:During Week 3 of the program, participants exclusively used CorelDRAW as the primary digital tool for Computer-Aided Design (CAD). CorelDRAW provided a versatile platform for creating precise vector graphics tailored for digital fabrication processes such as laser cutting and vinyl plotting. Its intuitive interface and powerful design features enabled participants to develop scalable designs, manage layers effectively, and prepare fabrication-ready files. The software’s capabilities in layout design, typography integration, and export formatting played a crucial role in bridging creative concepts with technical execution, making it an essential tool for the week’s learning outcomes.
  • Topics: During Week 3, the program focused on introducing participants to digital design principles using CorelDRAW. Key topics included vector-based drawing techniques, object manipulation, and layout composition for fabrication purposes. Participants explored how to use layers, shapes, and text tools effectively, while also learning how to prepare their designs for output on machines such as laser cutters and vinyl plotters. Emphasis was placed on understanding design constraints, file formatting, and the importance of precision in digital fabrication. The week also encouraged creative thinking and problem-solving through hands-on design challenges, reinforcing the role of CorelDRAW as a foundational tool in the digital fabrication workflow.
  • Key Concepts: Week 3 introduced participants to key concepts in digital design and fabrication using CorelDRAW. Central to the learning experience was the understanding of vector graphics, which form the foundation of precise and scalable design work.
  • Challenges Faced: One of the main challenges participants encountered during Week 3 was combining multiple design elements into a cohesive layout using CorelDRAW. This required careful alignment, grouping, and layering of objects, which proved difficult for those new to vector design. Another common challenge was adding color to drawings, especially understanding how fill and stroke settings affect the final output for fabrication.

Process Documentation

The process during Week 3 began with an introduction to CorelDRAW’s interface and basic tools, followed by guided exercises on creating vector shapes and organizing design elements. Participants practiced combining lines and shapes to form complete drawings, using grouping and alignment tools to maintain structure and precision. As the week progressed, they explored how to apply color fills and strokes, learning how these affect both visual presentation and machine interpretation during fabrication. Each task involved step-by-step design development—from sketching ideas digitally to refining layouts for export. Challenges such as merging lines correctly and applying color without disrupting cut paths were addressed through peer collaboration and instructor support. The process emphasized iterative learning, where participants improved their designs through feedback and hands-on experimentation.

    Reflection

    Reflecting on Week 3, it was inspiring to see how participants progressed from basic familiarity with CorelDRAW to confidently applying it in real-world digital fabrication contexts. The transition from simple sketches to structured, fabrication-ready designs highlighted their growing understanding of vector graphics and design principles. Despite facing challenges such as combining lines accurately and applying color without affecting cut paths, participants demonstrated resilience and a willingness to learn through trial and error. The week fostered not only technical growth but also creative confidence, as learners began to see how digital tools can transform ideas into tangible outcomes. This experience laid a strong foundation for future exploration in digital fabrication and design innovation.
